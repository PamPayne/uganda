# Q&A: Gambling Regulation in Uganda

Published on GamblingCompliance (https://gamblingcompliance.com)

21ST MAR 2019 | WRITTEN BY: ANDREA ELETTO

The Lotteries and Gaming Act 2016, along with five regulations, introduced a new legislative framework for the gambling sector in Uganda. Possible further developments could happen in the near future due to ongoing discussions about restricting the market to protect national companies and young people.

The gambling market in Uganda is regulated and local licences are available both for operators and suppliers.

The first laws providing regulation for gambling date back to the 1960s — the National Lotteries Act of 1967 and the Gaming and Pool Betting (Control and Taxation) Act of 1968.

The Lotteries and Gaming Act 2016 repealed the previous legislative framework and established the National Gaming Board (NGB) as the national gambling regulator, which is responsible for issuing licences for lotteries, casinos, gaming and betting. The full list of the types of licences available are specified in Article 27 of the act. The latest list of licensees, as of February 4, 2019, for all forms of gambling can be found here.

Sports betting is understood to be the most popular gambling activity in Uganda.

## 1. WHAT IS THE CURRENT STATUS OF THE GAMBLING MARKET?

The Lotteries and Gaming Act 2016, which came into effect on April 8, 2016 and repealed the previous legislation.

The act is supported by a set of five regulations, which came into effect on May 27, 2016:

- The Lotteries and Gaming (Betting) Regulations 2017 .
- The Lotteries and Gaming (Gaming and Betting Machines) Regulations 2017 .
- The Lotteries and Gaming (Licensing) Regulations 2017 .
- The Lotteries and Gaming (Fees) Regulations 2017 .
- The Lotteries and Gaming (Minimum Capital Requirements) Regulations 2017 .

The National Gaming Board Uganda (NGBU) is responsible for regulating and supervising the operation of lotteries, casinos and betting houses and for issuing licences. Its mandate also includes dispute resolution.

## 7. IS THERE A BLACKLIST OF UNLICENSED OPERATORS?

Casinos are regulated by the Lotteries and Gaming Act 2016 and local licences are available. Casino licensing is covered in Part V of the act, with specific conditions set out in Articles 28-33. Further rules regarding casino licensing are provided in Part III of the Lotteries and Gaming (Licensing) Regulations 2017; the necessary application forms are also attached to these regulations. General licensing conditions (applying to all licence types) can be found in Part VII of the act and Part VIII of the said regulations. Minimum capital requirements for each type of licence are set out in the Lotteries and Gaming (Minimum Capital Requirements) Regulations 2017.

## 8. DOES THE CURRENT LEGISLATION PROVIDE REGULATION FOR CASINOS?

Gaming machines are regulated by the Lotteries and Gaming Act 2016 and local licences are available both for B2C and B2B companies. Gaming and betting machine licensing is covered specifically in Part VI of the act. Gaming licensing is dealt with in further detail in Part IV of the Lotteries and Gaming (Licensing) Regulations 2017. Supplier licensing is specified in Part V of these regulations.

According to Article 35 of the act, “every gaming or betting machine or device shall be registered with the board in accordance with this act”. Article 36 of the act also established a national register of gaming or betting machines and devices “manufactured within or imported into Uganda”.

Article 37 of the act provides that “the board may, determine and set standards in respect of the manufacture, supply, installation, adaptation, maintenance or repair of gaming or betting machines”. According to the regulator’s website, national standards for sports betting and gaming equipment are still being developed. The consultation launched on the topic to receive comments from the industry ended in November 2017. No further information about the outcome of this consultation is available at this stage.

Technical standards are given in Articles 37 and 38 of the act. Rules relating to the registration, certification and testing of gaming machines are set out in the Lotteries and Gaming (Gaming and Betting Machines) Regulations 2017.

## 9. DOES THE CURRENT LEGISLATION PROVIDE REGULATION FOR GAMING MACHINES?

Lotteries are currently allowed and regulated in Uganda. There are two kinds of lotteries: the national lottery “conducted by or on behalf of the minister responsible for finance with the view of raising funds for a purpose defined by the minister”; and public lotteries, such as draw lotteries, bingo, instant lottery, lotto, keno or powerball and soccer football pool (Section 1 of the Lotteries and Gaming Act 2016).

Lotteries licensing (both for the national lottery and public lotteries) is covered in Part IV of the act. Further rules regarding lottery licensing are provided in Part II of the Lotteries and Gaming (Licensing) Regulations 2017; the necessary application forms are also attached to these regulations. General licensing conditions (applying to all licence types) can be found in Part VII of the act and Part VIII of the said regulations. Minimum capital requirements for each type of licence are set out in the Lotteries and Gaming (Minimum Capital Requirements) Regulations 2017.

## 10. WHAT IS THE CURRENT POSITION ON LOTTERIES?

Betting is allowed and regulated in Uganda. According to the national regulator, sports betting is the most popular activity. Betting may also be carried out on betting machines.

Betting licensing is covered in Parts V and VI of the act, with specific conditions set out in Articles 33-34. Further rules regarding betting licensing are provided in Part IV of the Lotteries and Gaming (Licensing) Regulations 2017; the necessary application forms are also attached to these regulations. General licensing conditions (applying to all licence types) can be found in Part VII of the act and Part VIII of the said regulations. Minimum capital requirements for each type of licence are set out in the Lotteries and Gaming (Minimum Capital Requirements) Regulations 2017.

## 11. WHAT IS THE CURRENT POSITION ON BETTING/BOOKMAKERS?

Racing is currently allowed and regulated in Uganda, being included among the activities that are defined as “betting” and “pool”, according to Section 1 of the Lotteries and Gaming Act 2016. Therefore, for further information, please refer to Question 11 above.

## 12. WHAT IS THE CURRENT POSITION ON RACING?

Both land-based and online poker are currently allowed and regulated in Uganda. Card games are included among the games allowed in casinos, defined as “a private club, or a room in a club, hotel, or other establishment, where gambling takes place or place where people gamble by playing card games, roulette, or slot machines which has been issued with a licence under this Act” (Section 1 of the Lotteries and Gaming Act, 2016). For further information, please refer to Questions 4 and 8 above.

## 13. WHAT IS THE CURRENT POSITION ON POKER?

The legal age for gambling in Uganda is 25 years of age (Article 1 of the Lotteries and Gaming Act 2016). Access to betting premises or facilities is permitted after age verification through the National Identification Card or passport (Regulation 5 of the Lotteries and Gaming (Betting) Regulations 2017 ).

## 14. WHAT IS THE LEGAL AGE FOR USING GAMING FACILITIES?

Licensing for authorised gaming activities is regulated by Part VII of the Lottery and Gaming Act 2016 and by the Lottery and Gaming (Licensing) Regulations 2017 .

General provisions on licensing are established by Part VII (Sections 39-47) of the Lottery and Gaming Act 2016 and Part VIII (Regulations 31-37) of the Lottery and Gaming (Licensing) Regulations 2017. - Specify the activity for which a licence is required and the type of licence requested.

- Where the applicant is a company, the age of the shareholders.
- Specify the address of the applicant.
- Be accompanied by the prescribed fee.

## 15. WHAT ARE THE CURRENT LICENSING REQUIREMENTS FOR AUTHORISED GAMING ACTIVITIES?

In determining the suitability of the applicant, the board shall take into account:

- “[T]he suitability of the applicant to carry on the activities for which the licence is required”.
- “[T]he integrity of the applicant or of a person relevant to the application”.
- “[T]he competence of the applicant or of a person relevant to the application to carry on the licensed activities in a manner consistent with this Act”.
- “[T]he financial and other circumstances of the applicant or of a person relevant to the application, including, the resources likely to be available for the purpose of carrying on the licensed activities.”

Further general licensing rule requirements are also included in Part VII of the Lottery and Gaming Act 2016 and in the Lottery and Gaming (Licensing) Regulations 2017. Further information about specific licensing requirements relating to individual types of gambling can be found in Questions 8-12 above.

According to the Lotteries and Gaming (Fees) Regulations 2017, licence fees vary from 10m Ugandan shillings (approximately £2,000) to 100m Ugandan shillings (approximately £20,000), depending on the type of game of chance and whether the licensee is a national or a foreigner. The exact amounts for each individual type of licence are set out in the Schedule to the said regulations.

## 16. WHAT IS THE COST OF A LICENCE OR AUTHORISATION?

According to Schedule 4 of the Lotteries and Gaming Act 2016 (as amended by the Lotteries and Gaming (Amendments) Act 2017, which came into force on July 1, 2017), the applicable taxation rate for authorised gaming activities in Uganda is 20 percent of gross gaming revenues, defined as ”the total amount of money staked less the payouts (winnings) for the period of filing returns”.

At the same time, according to the Income Tax (Amendment) Act 2017, players’ winnings from sports betting and pool betting are taxed at a rate of 15 percent through a withholding tax.

## 17. WHAT ARE THE APPLICABLE TAXATION RATES FOR THE AUTHORISED GAMING ACTIVITIES?

Every licensee must provide information on the danger of betting to people accessing betting activities, premises or facilities, in a manner determined by the gaming board. Not complying with this obligation is a criminal offence (Regulation 8 of the Lotteries and Gaming (Betting) Regulations 2017 ).

According to a research report published in 2016 by the Economic Policy Research Centre (EPRC), 5.7 percent of frequent gamblers (66.6 percent of all gamblers, which were 24.3 percent of the adult population) in Kampala City were classified as problem gamblers.

## 18. WHAT IS THE CURRENT POSITION ON PROBLEM GAMBLING? ARE THERE ANY KNOWN PREVALENCE GAMBLING SURVEYS?

According to Regulation 3 of the Lotteries and Gaming (Betting) Regulations 2017 , Uganda prohibits the promotion or advertisement of unlicensed “gaming or betting activit[ies]”. In addition, gaming or betting activities shall not be promoted or advertised in premises not approved by the gaming board, in a manner that is misleading or false, with a business name or slogan that is misleading or false, or in a manner that targets minors.

According to Regulation 3(4), a breach of this regulation is considered a criminal offence, punished with a “conviction to a fine not exceeding forty-eight currency points* or imprisonment not exceeding two years or both”.

Moreover, Section 65 of the act states that “[a]ny person who promotes or advertises a lottery which is not licensed under this act commits an offence and is liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding five hundred currency points or imprisonment not exceeding two years or both”.

\* One currency point is defined in Schedule 1 of the act as the equivalent of 20,000 Ugandan shillings.

## 19. DOES THE JURISDICTION PROHIBIT THE ADVERTISING OF GAMBLING PRODUCTS AND ACTIVITIES?

The Second Schedule (as amended by the Anti-Money Laundering (Amendment) Act 2017) of the Anti-Money Laundering Act 2013 includes both land-based and online casinos among the accountable persons that must identify clients, maintain records, record and report cash and monetary transactions, and report suspicious transactions.

## 20. WHAT AML LAWS AND REGULATIONS APPLY WITH REGARD TO GAMBLING ACTIVITIES?

According to Article 161(4) of the Penal Code, any person, other than a person who keeps a common gaming house, “who is found in a common gaming house shall be deemed, unless the contrary is proved, to be there for the purpose of unlawful gaming, and commits a misdemeanour and is liable to a fine of one thousand shillings for the first offence, and for each subsequent offence to a fine of three thousand shillings or to imprisonment for three months or to both such fine and imprisonment”.

According to Article 163(2) of the Criminal Code, “any person who, being the owner or occupier of any house, room or place, knowingly and wilfully permits it to be opened, kept or used as a common betting house by another person or who has the use or management, or assists in conducting the business, of a common betting house, commits a misdemeanour and is liable to imprisonment for one year”.

A common betting house is defined in Article 163(1) as “any house, room or place which is used for any of the following purposes:

- (a) for the purpose of bets being made in it between persons resorting to the place and—
  - (i) the owner, occupier or keeper of the place, or any person using the place;
  - (ii) any person procured or employed by or acting for or on behalf of any such owner, occupier or keeper, or person using the place; or any person having the care or management, or in any manner conducting the business, of the place; or
- (b) for the purpose of any money or other property being paid or received there by or on behalf of any such owner, occupier or keeper, or person using the place, as, or for the consideration
  - (i) for an assurance, undertaking, promise or agreement, express or implied, to pay or give thereafter any money or other property on any event or contingency of or relating to any horse race or other race, fight, game, sport or exercise; or
  - (ii) for securing the paying or giving by some other person of any money or other property on any such event or contingency”.

According to Section 67(1) of the Lotteries and Gaming Act 2016, “a person who (a) operates a casino; (b) provides facilities for the playing of bingo; (c) makes a gaming and betting machine available for use; (d) provides other facilities for gaming and betting; or (e) provide facilities for betting, whether by making or accepting bets, by acting as a betting intermediary or by providing other facilities for the making or accepting of bets; without a licence, commits an offence and is liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding forty eight currency points or imprisonment not exceeding two years or both”.
